Today, we're going to learn about **Numbers 1 to 10** and how to count simple things! Knowing numbers is super useful for everyday life, and it's a fun way to practice reading and pronunciation.

## Lesson

1.  **Numbers in Arabic**
    *   Just like in English, Arabic has its own set of numerals (the symbols like '1', '2', '3') and words for those numbers.
    *   Interestingly, while Arabic text is written from right to left, Arabic **numerals** are written and read from **left to right**, just like in English! So, `١٢٣` is '123'.

2.  **Counting from 1 to 10**
    *   When we learn numbers in Arabic, it's important to know that numbers sometimes change based on the gender of the noun they are counting. For today, we will introduce the most common form for counting, and for numbers 1 and 2, we will also show their feminine forms. For 3-10, we'll keep it simple for now, as the full rules can be a bit tricky for beginners.

    Let's meet the numbers!

    *   **1: وَاحِدٌ (waaḥidun)**
        *   Numeral: ١
        *   Used for masculine nouns.
        *   Feminine form: **وَاحِدَةٌ (waaḥidatun)** (for feminine nouns)
    *   **2: اِثْنَانِ (ithnāni)**
        *   Numeral: ٢
        *   Used for masculine nouns.
        *   Feminine form: **اِثْنَتَانِ (ithnatāni)** (for feminine nouns)
    *   **3: ثَلَاثَةٌ (thalāthatun)**
        *   Numeral: ٣
    *   **4: أَرْبَعَةٌ (arbaʿatun)**
        *   Numeral: ٤
    *   **5: خَمْسَةٌ (khamsatun)**
        *   Numeral: ٥
    *   **6: سِتَّةٌ (sittatun)**
        *   Numeral: ٦
    *   **7: سَبْعَةٌ (sabʿatun)**
        *   Numeral: ٧
    *   **8: ثَمَانِيَةٌ (thamāniyatun)**
        *   Numeral: ٨
    *   **9: تِسْعَةٌ (tisʿatun)**
        *   Numeral: ٩
    *   **10: عَشَرَةٌ (ʿasharatun)**
        *   Numeral: ١٠

    You'll notice that many of these numbers (3-10 as listed here) end with the تَاء مَرْبُوطَة (ة). This is the standard form when counting masculine items in Arabic, but don't worry about that rule yet. For now, just focus on recognizing each number.

## Examples

Let's practice reading and writing these numbers. We'll also try counting some of the nouns you already know!

**The Numbers:**

1.  **١ - وَاحِدٌ** (waaḥidun) - one (masculine)
2.  **٢ - اِثْنَانِ** (ithnāni) - two (masculine)
3.  **٣ - ثَلَاثَةٌ** (thalāthatun) - three
4.  **٤ - أَرْبَعَةٌ** (arbaʿatun) - four
5.  **٥ - خَمْسَةٌ** (khamsatun) - five
6.  **٦ - سِتَّةٌ** (sittatun) - six
7.  **٧ - سَبْعَةٌ** (sabʿatun) - seven
8.  **٨ - ثَمَانِيَةٌ** (thamāniyatun) - eight
9.  **٩ - تِسْعَةٌ** (tisʿatun) - nine
10. **١٠ - عَشَرَةٌ** (ʿasharatun) - ten

**Counting with Nouns:**

*   **One book:** `كِتَابٌ وَاحِدٌ.` (kitaabun waaḥidun.)
    *   *Notice*: `كِتَابٌ` is masculine, so `وَاحِدٌ` is used and matches in gender and indefiniteness.
*   **One school:** `مَدْرَسَةٌ وَاحِدَةٌ.` (madrasatun waaḥidatun.)
    *   *Notice*: `مَدْرَسَةٌ` is feminine, so `وَاحِدَةٌ` is used and matches in gender and indefiniteness.
*   **Two pens:** `قَلَمَانِ اِثْنَانِ.` (qalamāni ithnāni.)
    *   *Note*: Nouns for two items usually end in `ـَانِ` or `ـَيْنِ`. We'll cover this later. For now, just recognize `اِثْنَانِ`.
*   **Two cars:** `سَيَّارَتَانِ اِثْنَتَانِ.` (sayyaaratāni ithnatāni.)
    *   *Note*: `سَيَّارَةٌ` (car) is feminine, so we use `اِثْنَتَانِ`.
*   **Three books:** `ثَلَاثَةُ كُتُبٍ.` (thalāthatu kutubin.)
    *   *Note*: For numbers 3-10, the noun being counted is usually plural and takes a Kasra. For today, just focus on `ثَلَاثَةُ` meaning 'three'. We'll introduce plural nouns much later.
*   **Four pens:** `أَرْبَعَةُ أَقْلَامٍ.` (arbaʿatu aqlāmin.)
*   **Five schools:** `خَمْسُ مَدَارِسَ.` (khamsu madārisa.)
*   **Six cars:** `سِتُّ سَيَّارَاتٍ.` (sittu sayyārātin.)

Don't worry about the complex grammar rules for numbers 3-10 yet (like the form of the noun after them or the gender of the number itself). For now, the most important thing is to simply memorize the Arabic word and numeral for each number from 1 to 10. We will revisit the intricacies of counting much later!
