Today, we're going to learn how to **describe nouns**! We'll introduce **adjectives** – words like "new," "big," or "small" – and discover the special rules about how they must **match** the noun they describe. This is a super important concept in Arabic!

## Lesson

1.  **What is an Adjective?**
    *   An adjective is a word that describes a noun. For example, in "a *red* car," "red" is the adjective describing "car."
    *   In Arabic, adjectives come **after** the noun they describe. This is different from English, where the adjective usually comes before the noun ("new book" vs. "book new").

2.  **Adjective Matching Rules (The Four Agreements)**
    *   This is the most important part of today's lesson! In Arabic, an adjective must **always match** the noun it describes in four ways:
        1.  **Gender (Masculine / Feminine)**:
            *   If the noun is **masculine**, the adjective must also be **masculine**.
            *   If the noun is **feminine** (ends in تَاء مَرْبُوطَة ة), the adjective must also be **feminine** (and will usually end in تَاء مَرْبُوطَة ة).
        2.  **Definiteness (Definite / Indefinite)**:
            *   If the noun is **definite** (has `الْـ` at the beginning), the adjective must also be **definite** (and have `الْـ` at the beginning).
            *   If the noun is **indefinite** (has `التَّنْوِينُ` at the end), the adjective must also be **indefinite** (and have `التَّنْوِينُ` at the end).
        3.  **Number (Singular / Plural)**:
            *   For now, we will only use **singular** nouns and **singular** adjectives. Later, you'll learn about plural forms.
        4.  **Case (Ending Vowel)**:
            *   If the noun ends with a **Damma ( ُ )** or **Tanween Damma ( ٌ )**, the adjective will also end with a **Damma ( ُ )** or **Tanween Damma ( ٌ )**.
            *   If the noun ends with a **Kasra ( ِ )** or **Tanween Kasra ( ٍ )**, the adjective will also end with a **Kasra ( ِ )** or **Tanween Kasra ( ٍ )**.
            *   If the noun ends with a **Fatha ( َ )** or **Tanween Fatha ( ً )**, the adjective will also end with a **Fatha ( َ )** or **Tanween Fatha ( ً )**.
            *   For our examples today, we will mostly focus on the Damma/Tanween Damma case, as that is what you've seen for subjects and indefinite predicates.

    *   **Important Distinction**: When an adjective describes a noun (e.g., "a new book"), the adjective comes *after* the noun, and **both** words will follow these matching rules. This is **not** an "X is Y" sentence. The "is" is *not* implied. It's just a descriptive phrase.

3.  **Introducing New Adjectives**

    Let's learn some common adjectives and their masculine/feminine forms:

    *   **جَدِيدٌ (jadeedun)**: new (masculine)
    *   **جَدِيدَةٌ (jadeedatun)**: new (feminine)
    *   **كَبِيرٌ (kabeerun)**: big (masculine)
    *   **كَبِيرَةٌ (kabeeratun)**: big (feminine)
    *   **صَغِيرٌ (sagheerun)**: small (masculine)
    *   **صَغِيرَةٌ (sagheeratun)**: small (feminine)
    *   **جَمِيلٌ (jameelun)**: beautiful (masculine)
    *   **جَمِيلَةٌ (jameelatun)**: beautiful (feminine)

## Examples

Let's put these rules into practice with words you already know! Pay close attention to how the adjective always matches the noun in gender, definiteness, and ending vowel.

**1. Indefinite Noun + Indefinite Adjective ("a new book", "a big school")**
*   Both the noun and adjective will have `التَّنْوِينُ` (Tanween Damma ٌ).

    *   **A new book.** (`كِتَابٌ` is masculine)
        *   `كِتَابٌ جَدِيدٌ.` (kitaabun jadeedun)
            *   *Notice*: `كِتَابٌ` (masc, indef, Tanween Damma), `جَدِيدٌ` (masc, indef, Tanween Damma). They match!
    *   **A new school.** (`مَدْرَسَةٌ` is feminine)
        *   `مَدْرَسَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ.` (madrasatun jadeedatun)
            *   *Notice*: `مَدْرَسَةٌ` (fem, indef, Tanween Damma), `جَدِيدَةٌ` (fem, indef, Tanween Damma). They match!
    *   **A big pen.** (`قَلَمٌ` is masculine)
        *   `قَلَمٌ كَبِيرٌ.` (qalamun kabeerun)
    *   **A small car.** (`سَيَّارَةٌ` is feminine)
        *   `سَيَّارَةٌ صَغِيرَةٌ.` (sayyaaratun sagheeratun)
    *   **A beautiful room.** (`غُرْفَةٌ` is feminine)
        *   `غُرْفَةٌ جَمِيلَةٌ.` (ghurfatun jameelatun)

**2. Definite Noun + Definite Adjective ("the new book", "the big school")**
*   Both the noun and adjective will have `الْـ` at the beginning and a single Damma ( ُ ) at the end.

    *   **The new book.** (`الْكِتَابُ` is masculine)
        *   `الْكِتَابُ الْجَدِيدُ.` (al-kitaabu al-jadeedu)
            *   *Notice*: `الْكِتَابُ` (masc, def, Damma), `الْجَدِيدُ` (masc, def, Damma). They match!
    *   **The new school.** (`الْمَدْرَسَةُ` is feminine)
        *   `الْمَدْرَسَةُ الْجَدِيدَةُ.` (al-madrasatu al-jadeedatu)
            *   *Notice*: `الْمَدْرَسَةُ` (fem, def, Damma), `الْجَدِيدَةُ` (fem, def, Damma). They match!
    *   **The big pen.** (`الْقَلَمُ` is masculine)
        *   `الْقَلَمُ الْكَبِيرُ.` (al-qalamu al-kabeeru)
    *   **The small car.** (`السَّيَّارَةُ` is feminine)
        *   `السَّيَّارَةُ الصَّغِيرَةُ.` (as-sayyaaratu as-sagheeratu)
            *   *Remember Sun Letters for `السَّيَّارَةُ` and `الصَّغِيرَةُ`! The 'L' is silent, and the first letter is doubled.*

**3. Using Adjectives in Nominal Sentences (X is Y)**
*   When an adjective is used as the **Predicate (الْخَبَرُ)** of a nominal sentence (e.g., "The book is new"), it follows the rules for predicates we learned on Day 6: it must be **indefinite** (with Tanween Damma ٌ) and match the subject's **gender**.

    *   **The book is new.** (`الْكِتَابُ` is masculine, subject)
        *   `الْكِتَابُ جَدِيدٌ.` (al-kitaabu jadeedun.)
            *   *Notice*: `الْكِتَابُ` (definite subject), `جَدِيدٌ` (indefinite predicate, masculine matching `الْكِتَابُ`).
    *   **The school is big.** (`الْمَدْرَسَةُ` is feminine, subject)
        *   `الْمَدْرَسَةُ كَبِيرَةٌ.` (al-madrasatu kabeeratun.)
            *   *Notice*: `الْمَدْرَسَةُ` (definite subject), `كَبِيرَةٌ` (indefinite predicate, feminine matching `الْمَدْرَسَةُ`).

This might seem like a lot of rules, but the key is practice! Adjectives are everywhere in Arabic, and once you get used to these matching rules, you'll start seeing them naturally.
